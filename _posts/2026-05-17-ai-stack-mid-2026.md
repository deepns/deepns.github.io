About 18 months ago I wrote a post called [Building Blocks of a RAG Pipeline](/tech/rag-building-blocks/) that kicked off what became an unexpected series. I was trying to make sense of a fast-moving space by writing about it. The plan was maybe four or five posts. It turned into twenty.

This is the capstone. I want to look back at what I got right, what surprised me, and what the stack actually looks like for people building with LLMs today — not the hype version, but what's running in production at teams I've talked to and read about.

---

## What I Got Right

**RAG became the baseline, not the ceiling.** When I started writing about this, "do you use RAG?" was still a genuine question. Now it's assumed. The question is which flavor — naive chunking, hybrid search, agentic retrieval, graph-augmented. The fundamentals I wrote about in the foundation post haven't changed. The sophistication layered on top has.

**pgvector quietly won a large chunk of the market.** I wrote about this in February 2025 and got pushback in the comments along the lines of "real applications need a dedicated vector DB." The reality is that a lot of teams already running Postgres chose pgvector because operational simplicity matters more than marginal performance gains. pgvector with HNSW indexing is genuinely good enough for most workloads under ~10M vectors.

**Observability went from afterthought to requirement.** The OpenTelemetry post was the one I wasn't sure was worth writing — it felt too infrastructure-y for an AI-focused series. It turned out to be the most practically useful one. You cannot debug a production RAG pipeline without traces. You can't.

---

## What Surprised Me

**Prompt engineering stayed relevant longer than expected.** In late 2024 there was a lot of "models are getting so good that prompting won't matter." Models did get better. Prompting still matters — a lot. The better the model, the more leverage you get from a well-structured prompt. The discipline of treating prompts as versioned, tested artifacts (the gateway post pattern) has only become more standard.

**The LLM API market consolidated faster than I thought.** The landscape of providers that meaningfully compete at production quality has gotten smaller, not larger. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google account for most of the production API usage. The open model ecosystem (running via Ollama, vLLM, etc.) is genuinely competitive now, but it's a separate market segment — teams with the ops maturity to run their own inference.

**Evaluation is still the unsolved problem.** I wrote the LLMOps evaluation post with some optimism that frameworks like RAGAS and LLM-as-judge were getting mature enough to build reliable eval pipelines around. They have gotten better. It's still fundamentally hard. The "eval your eval" problem is real — you end up using an LLM to judge whether your LLM is doing well, and the whole thing is turtles all the way down.

## The Stack in Mid-2026

For a new greenfield project starting today, here's what I'd reach for:

**Embeddings**: OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small` for most cases. If you need local or privacy-constrained, `nomic-embed-text` via Ollama is a reasonable choice.

**Vector store**: pgvector if you already have Postgres. Qdrant for a dedicated vector DB (especially if you need hybrid search or relevance feedback). Pinecone if you want zero ops and can afford it.

**Orchestration**: LangChain for straightforward RAG pipelines. LangGraph once you need anything with conditional logic, loops, or multiple agents. Haystack if you're building a product where other people will configure pipelines.

**Serving**: FastAPI for bespoke services. BentoML if you're packaging ML models specifically and want the batching/runner abstractions.

**Observability**: OpenTelemetry + whatever backend your org already uses. If greenfield, Langfuse has become the go-to for LLM-specific observability — it sits on top of OTEL and adds prompt tracking, eval integration, and cost dashboards.

**Evaluation**: RAGAS for RAG-specific metrics. For general LLM eval, PromptFoo has gotten good. Running evals in CI with a golden dataset is the practice, even if imperfect.

**Cost control**: Semantic caching for high-repetition use cases. Complexity routing (cheap model for simple queries) almost always pays for itself. Track costs per request from day one.

## What I'd Do Differently

Start with evaluation infrastructure earlier. Every project I've worked on or read about has the same pattern: build the thing, ship it, wonder if it's working, scramble to add eval later. Eval is hard to retrofit. Build a small golden test set on day one, even if it's 20 questions. Run it on every change.

Also: pick boring tools for the non-AI parts of your stack. The AI components are already plenty of new technology to manage. Don't compound it by also trying out a new database, new language, new container orchestrator. The teams I've seen succeed treat the LLM integration as the exotic ingredient and everything else as commodity.

---

## What's Next

I don't know exactly what the next 18 months of posts looks like. The space keeps moving. A few things I'm watching:

- **Inference-time compute** (OpenAI's o-series approach, thinking tokens) changing the cost/quality curve in ways that break existing mental models
- **Compound AI systems** that chain multiple models together getting more sophisticated
- **The eval problem** actually getting solved (I'm skeptical but hopeful)

Thanks for following along with this series. Writing these posts has been the most reliable way I've found to actually learn something — you don't really understand a tool until you try to explain it clearly enough that someone else could use it.
